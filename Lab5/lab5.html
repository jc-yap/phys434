Brief description of experiment
In order to make sense of the data we are given, we briefly outline key parts of the experiment in the Large Hadron Collider with the help of the diagram below.
The LHC is a tube ring that accelerates particles along the beam line. The ATLAS detector is a cylinder in the ring that particles are made to collide in, at the interaction point. It collects data about the collisions. Upon collision, new particles may be created and split off from the beam line. Some new particles are short lived, and do not travel that far before decaying into other particles, which then hit the detector as jets. Based on the characteristics of the jet (particle grouping, mass, angle, etc.), experimenters can trace its path back to where the particle decayed and determine its mass. The Higgs boson is known to decay into a bottom quark-antiquark () pair, which produce a 2-prong substructure in the jet. Many of the jet characteristics are variables given to us in the data. The coordinate system used gives us the angles as defined below.
φ is the azimuthal angle about the z-axis and θ is the polar angle.

A scatter plot comparison of mass against transverse momentum for QCD background and Higgs particles shows QCD particles tightly-grouped in the lower-right corner and spread out towards the upper-left, while Higgs data shows particles grouped at the upper and right edges of the plot, and spread out towards the middle. The axes on both plots are different, so their behavior would be indistinguishable if the plots were superimposed, but the different patterns are distinuishable nonetheless.
Since there are many possible combinations of variables to plot, we save time by looking in the literature for quantities that differentiate QCD background from Higgs particles. Two effective discriminating variables for two-prong objects are the ratio of 2-subjettiness to 1-subjettiness, [1] and the ratio of 2-point and 3-point ECFs [2]. The latter is computed by inverting the d2 variable we are given.
